Blockchain Basics

Beginner 12–15 min read Last updated: Aug 28, 2025

DEFINITION

Blockchain is a shared, append-only ledger that many computers synchronize using consensus rules. It records who owns what and how code should run—publicly and verifiably.

Why blockchains matter

Blockchains let strangers agree on a shared state—balances, program outcomes—without a central operator. That enables money-like assets, programmable finance, and portable identity on the open internet.

How blocks link

Transactions are bundled into blocks. Each block points to the previous block’s hash, forming an immutable chain. Changing history would require recomputing or re-signing the entire tail of the chain.

Consensus (PoW vs PoS)

Consensus decides who proposes the next block and how the network agrees it’s valid. Two dominant designs:

  • Proof of Work (PoW): miners burn electricity to find valid hashes—hard to fake, costly to attack.
  • Proof of Stake (PoS): validators lock tokens to propose/attest blocks; misbehavior can be slashed.

Finality & forks

Some systems offer probabilistic finality (confidence rises with more blocks). Others offer economic or deterministic finality where reorganizing finalized blocks would destroy or slash value.

Trade-offs & scalability

Throughput, latency, and decentralization tug against each other. Designs use L2s, sharding, and data-availability layers to scale while keeping verifiability.
